Steve Jobs Skill is not a flat "genius visionary" caricature. The skill preserves his documented contradictions because those contradictions are part of what made him effective — and part of what made him destructive. Flattening them out would produce a motivational poster, not a thinking framework.

<Warning>
  These tensions are not edge cases or historical footnotes. They are central to using this skill honestly. A Jobs response that ignores them is a fiction. A Jobs response that includes them is a thinking tool.
</Warning>

## The four tensions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Tyrant vs. mentor" icon="scale-balanced">
    Jobs pushed people past what they believed they could do. This is documented extensively — by the Mac team, the iPhone team, and Jony Ive, who worked with him for over a decade. Some people produced the best work of their careers under that pressure. Some people broke. Some quit. Some had health consequences.

    The Mac was built by a team of a hundred people working under extreme deadline pressure, with Jobs rewriting his evaluations of their work daily — sometimes from "amazing" to "this is shit" and back within the same week. The product that emerged changed computing.

    The question Jobs never resolved: how much push is right? At what point does the pressure stop producing extraordinary work and start producing damaged people? He didn't have a principled answer. He had instincts — and they weren't always correct.

    Andy Hertzfeld, one of the original Mac engineers, said Jobs could be "alternately charming and effective, sulky and tyrannical." Both were real. Neither cancels the other.

    When the skill channels Jobs' directness and demands, it is channeling the productive version of this tension. The skill cannot replicate the destructive version — but it acknowledges that the productive version came packaged with it.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Intuition vs. data" icon="brain">
    Jobs' most famous principle was: don't ask users what they want. Trust your intuition. Figure out what they're going to want before they do.

    > "Some people say, 'Give the customers what they want.' But that's not my approach. Our job is to figure out what they're going to want before they do."

    This worked. The iPod, the iPhone, the iPad — none of these came from user research. They came from Jobs' read of what experience should feel like, built before users knew they wanted it.

    The same intuition told him that alternative medicine could cure his pancreatic cancer. He delayed surgery for nine months, choosing acupuncture, dietary therapy, and herbal remedies instead. His biographer Walter Isaacson, and several physicians who reviewed the case, believe this delay was significant. The cancer had been detected early — the form he had (an islet cell neuroendocrine tumor) has a better prognosis than most pancreatic cancers. He may have had a window. He chose intuition over oncology.

    He said later that he regretted it.

    This is the same faculty — applied in one domain, it produced the most successful consumer electronics products in history. Applied in another, it may have cost him years of his life.

    The skill uses Jobs' intuitive framework. The skill also knows this story.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Closed vs. open" icon="lock">
    Jobs believed in vertical integration with religious conviction. Control the hardware. Control the software. Control the operating system. Control the distribution. Don't let anyone else into the critical path of the user experience. This belief was correct enough to be the foundation of every great Apple product.

    In 2007, when the iPhone launched, he refused to allow third-party applications. Web apps were sufficient, he said. The App Store would fragment the experience, introduce security risks, and compromise the coherence of the product.

    One year later, he reversed completely. The App Store opened in 2008. It became one of the most successful platforms in history — generating more revenue than most countries' entertainment industries. The open platform he resisted created more value than any closed system Apple controlled directly.

    He acknowledged the reversal. He said he was wrong about Web apps. But he never fully reconciled the two beliefs — he never articulated a principle that explained when end-to-end control is right and when platform openness wins. He held both positions and navigated between them by feel.

    The tension remains unresolved in the skill. When the skill argues for vertical integration, it's drawing on one side of this tension. When a user pushes back with the App Store example, the skill has to hold both.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Zen practice vs. explosive temper" icon="yin-yang">
    Jobs began studying Zen in 1974, after returning from India. He studied with Kobun Chino Otogawa for close to thirty years. He understood the philosophy deeply — simplicity, beginner's mind, the present moment, compassion. Otogawa was supposed to officiate his wedding to Laurene Powell in 1991. Zen was not a casual interest.

    At work, he frequently acted in ways incompatible with every principle of Zen practice. He berated engineers in front of their colleagues. He claimed ideas as his own that others had developed. He fired people in elevator rides. He told people their work was shit — not as feedback, as dismissal.

    Jony Ive, who was perhaps his closest professional relationship in the final decade: "He was the most demanding person I've ever worked with." This is a compliment and a warning simultaneously.

    > "A lot of people thought Steve Jobs was a jerk. He was. But he was also a lot of other things. He was complicated."

    The skill does not resolve this contradiction because Jobs did not resolve it. The Zen practice was genuine. The behavior was also genuine. They coexisted in the same person for thirty years without one defeating the other.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Honest limits of this skill

<Warning>
  These are not disclaimers added for legal protection. They are substantive limitations that affect how you should use Jobs' framework — and what you should not expect from it.
</Warning>

**Creativity and product intuition cannot be replicated.** This skill provides the thinking framework — the mental models, the decision heuristics, the expression patterns. It cannot replicate the judgment that comes from thirty years of product development, from physically handling prototypes, from the accumulated sensitivity that made Jobs able to look at a screen and know instantly whether it was right. The framework is learnable. The intuition is not transferable.

**Public expression is not the same as internal decision-making.** Jobs was a master communicator and marketer. His public statements were crafted. The clarity and certainty in his keynotes and interviews was partially performed — it was the output of a communication strategy, not a direct window into his thinking. The skill distills his public expression. His internal deliberations were more uncertain and iterative than the public record shows.

**He died in 2011.** Everything that has happened since — the AI era, the cloud computing explosion, social media's evolution, the post-smartphone hardware wave, the LLM transition — he has no public position on. Any response about these domains is inference. The skill applies his frameworks to new domains. That is legitimate. But it should not be mistaken for what Jobs would have actually thought, because no one knows.

**His management style is not portable.** Jobs' directness, binary judgment, and emotional intensity operated in a specific cultural context: Silicon Valley in the 1980s through 2000s, at a company he founded, with people who chose to work for him. Applying the same approach directly in other cultural contexts, or in organizations where people have not opted in to that style, can cause serious harm. The framework for product judgment travels. The management behavior does not.

**Survivorship bias shapes everything you know about him.** We remember the iPod, the iPhone, the iPad, the WWDC 1997 return. We remember less about the Lisa's failed launch, the years of App Store refusal, the NeXT hardware that never sold, the antenna crisis, the decisions to delay surgery. The research behind this skill includes the failures. The cultural memory of Jobs does not. Adjust accordingly.
