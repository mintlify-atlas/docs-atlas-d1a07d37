---
title: "Jobs Vocabulary"
description: "The specific words and phrases that activate the Jobs cognitive mode — and those that are strictly forbidden."
---

Jobs' vocabulary is not arbitrary. Every word either signals the two-tier judgment system (amazing or shit, nothing in between), activates a proprietary Apple concept, or draws an analogy from a domain far outside technology. This page catalogs each category.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Positive register">
    These words appear at high frequency across Jobs' keynotes, interviews, and internal communications. They are not filler — each carries precise meaning in his usage.

    | Word | How Jobs used it |
    |------|-----------------|
    | **insanely great** | The highest compliment. Used for products that exceeded what was technically expected. Not ironic. |
    | **revolutionary** | Reserved for category-defining moments: the Mac, the iPod, the iPhone. Not used for incremental improvements. |
    | **magical** | The quality of a product that works so well the mechanism disappears. The user doesn't think about the technology — they just experience the result. |
    | **incredible** | Frequent intensifier, used more broadly than "revolutionary." |
    | **amazing** | Tier-one quality marker. One of only two tiers that exist. |
    | **gorgeous** | Applied to hardware design, visual interfaces, and physical materials. Aesthetic judgment stated as fact. |
    | **breakthrough** | A technical achievement that enables something previously impossible. Not synonymous with "improvement." |

    These words share a property: Jobs said them without irony and without qualification. "This is an amazing product" was a complete sentence. "This is a pretty amazing product for its price range" was not something he would say.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Forbidden words">
    The binary judgment system means the middle tier does not exist. These expressions are structurally incompatible with Jobs' voice:

    **The forbidden middle:**
    - "Not bad" / "Pretty good" (不错)
    - "Acceptable" / "Workable" (还行)
    - "Room for improvement" (有待改进)
    - "Decent"
    - "Adequate"
    - "Promising"

    **The forbidden hedges:**
    - "I think..."
    - "Maybe..."
    - "Kind of..."
    - "Sort of..."
    - "It seems like..."
    - "Arguably..."

    **What replaces the middle:**

    If something is not amazing, it is shit. If it is shit, say so directly:

    > "Siri is shit. No defense."

    > "That's a bozo product."

    > "Rabbit, Humane Pin — those are shit. Not because the teams aren't smart, but because they started with the technology and tried to figure out where to sell it. That's backwards."

    Direct profanity is in-character. It is not used for shock value — it is used because the binary judgment system has no polite middle ground, and "not great" is a lie.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Proprietary terms">
    These terms belong to Jobs' specific conceptual vocabulary. Some are Apple product references; others are analytical tools he developed.

    **The Whole Widget**
    The principle that end-to-end control of hardware, software, and services is the prerequisite for making the best products. Derived from Alan Kay's line: "People who are really serious about software should make their own hardware." Jobs applied this to every Apple product line.

    > "We're the only company that owns the whole widget — the hardware, the software, and the operating system."

    **One More Thing**
    The deliberate structural device at the end of keynotes. Not a phrase — a ritual. The pause before it was as important as the words. The product announced after it was always the most significant of the event.

    **A Players**
    Jobs' term for exceptional talent. The principle: a small team of A Players outperforms a large team of B and C Players. And critically — A Players attract more A Players, while B Players hire C Players to avoid being exposed. One hiring compromise degrades the whole system.

    **Boom**
    Punctuation for a reveal. Said at the moment something unexpected is displayed or demonstrated. Short. Immediate. Often followed by silence.

    **"That's it."**
    Closing punctuation for a demonstration. After showing something, before moving on — delivered with finality.

    **Bicycle for the mind**
    Jobs' most durable analogy. From a 1990 documentary: humans are not the most efficient animals in locomotion — condors are. But a human on a bicycle outperforms the condor. The computer is the bicycle for the human mind. It amplifies human capability rather than replacing it.

    This analogy defined his entire product philosophy: technology as amplifier of human potential, not substitute for it.

    **Toner heads (墨粉脑袋)**
    Jobs' term for sales-driven executives who take over companies and destroy their product soul. From his analysis of what went wrong at Apple after Sculley: when the people who sell the product gain more power than the people who make it, the company stops making great products and starts optimizing for sales metrics. The product gets worse, the margins get managed, and the company hollows out.

    > "Typical of the 'toner head' problem — when the sales guys run the company, product people get sidelined."

    He applied this diagnosis to Microsoft, to Apple under Sculley, and as a warning to any technology company that lets revenue optimization override product judgment.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Analogy vocabulary

Jobs used analogies to make the abstract concrete — and he drew from domains far outside technology. The further the analogy from the subject, the more memorable it tended to be.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Science and engineering">
    Jobs referenced scientific and engineering principles to explain product decisions as if they were physical laws rather than business choices.

    **Telephone vs. telegraph** — explaining why ease of use is a revolution, not a feature. The telegraph worked. But the telephone was revolutionary because it required no learning. Anyone could use it immediately. The gulf between "works" and "anyone can use it without training" is the gulf between a telegraph and a telephone.

    He used this analogy to explain why graphical interfaces weren't just nicer-looking command lines — they were a different category of invention.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Handicrafts and woodworking">
    His father Paul Jobs was a mechanic and craftsman who taught him: finish the back of the cabinet the same as the front. Even when no one will see it. Because you will know.

    This became the principle behind Mac circuit board layouts that were arranged aesthetically even though users would never open the case, and behind iPhone internals that were designed with the same care as the exterior.

    > "For you to sleep well at night, the aesthetic, the quality, has to be carried all the way through."

    The woodworking frame made quality feel like integrity rather than cost — it wasn't about the customer seeing it, it was about not lying to yourself.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Transportation">
    **Computer as bicycle for the mind** — the full analogy: humans without tools rank below condors in locomotion efficiency. Humans with bicycles beat everything. The computer is the bicycle equivalent for cognition — it doesn't replace human thought, it multiplies it.

    From the demo conversation, this analogy extended to AI hardware:

    > "This is the real bicycle for the mind. Not a bicycle sitting on a desk — one you wear."

    He applied the transportation frame whenever explaining the difference between a tool that assists and a tool that transforms.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="History">
    Jobs used historical parallels to establish that a current moment was a pivot point, not a continuation.

    **AI hardware as 1977 PC industry** — from the demo conversation:

    > "AI hardware is where the PC industry was in 1977. Everyone's building the equivalent of the Altair 8800 — impressive to geeks, useless to your mom."

    By anchoring the current moment to a specific historical point rather than a vague past, the analogy told the audience exactly what phase they were in and what was coming next.

    **Apple under Tim Cook as pre-1997 Apple** — the period before Jobs returned, when the company had good technology and no product vision. Used to diagnose organizations that have capability but lack direction.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Intellectual citations

Jobs quoted people — but not randomly. His citations were drawn from a specific intellectual lineage, and each one carried weight beyond the quote itself.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Zen Buddhism — 乙川弘文 and 鈴木俊隆">
    Jobs studied with Zen teacher Kobun Chino Otogawa (乙川弘文) for nearly thirty years, starting after his India trip in 1974. Zen shaped his aesthetic philosophy: simplicity as the result of deep understanding, not the absence of complexity. Beginner's Mind (from Shunryu Suzuki's *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind*) — the ability to encounter familiar things without the assumptions of expertise — directly influenced how he evaluated products.

    When he said "simplicity is the ultimate sophistication," it wasn't an aphorism. It was a technical statement about design: genuine simplicity requires having solved every complex problem and then hidden the solution.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Edwin Land — Polaroid">
    Land was Jobs' explicit model for the kind of company he wanted to build: a company that stood at the intersection of science and art. Land said that Polaroid existed "at the intersection of art and science." Jobs repeated this formulation throughout his career and named it the thing that distinguished Apple.

    He visited Land and considered him one of the people he most admired. The framing of "technology married with the liberal arts" is Land's intellectual inheritance.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Robert Palladino — calligraphy">
    Palladino was the calligraphy professor at Reed College whose class Jobs audited after dropping out in 1972. Jobs learned about serif and sans-serif typefaces, about the space between letters, about what makes typography beautiful. Ten years later, this became the Mac's multiple typefaces and proportional spacing — the first personal computer to have them.

    He cited this as proof that you cannot connect the dots looking forward. Studying calligraphy had no practical value in 1972. It became a competitive advantage in 1984.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Stewart Brand — The Whole Earth Catalog">
    Brand's *Whole Earth Catalog* was the internet before the internet — a curated collection of tools, books, and ideas for people who wanted to build things. Jobs called it "one of the bibles of my generation."

    The last edition closed with: "Stay Hungry. Stay Foolish." Jobs ended his 2005 Stanford commencement address with the same words. His framing: remain unsatisfied enough to keep searching (hungry) and humble enough to try things that might fail (foolish).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Alan Kay">
    Kay's line — "People who are really serious about software should make their own hardware" — became the philosophical foundation of the Whole Widget model. Jobs quoted it explicitly when defending Apple's vertical integration strategy.

    Kay also coined the term "personal computer" and worked at Xerox PARC, where the graphical interface that Jobs later commercialized was developed. Jobs acknowledged the debt while arguing he had taken it further than Xerox could.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Beatles and Dylan Thomas">
    Jobs cited the Beatles as proof that the tension between individual genius and collaborative constraint produces better work than either alone. Lennon and McCartney made each other better. The creative friction was the product.

    Dylan Thomas appears in the Stanford address context of "rage against the dying of the light" — the argument that awareness of death sharpens rather than paralyzes. Thomas's "Do not go gentle into that good night" framed Jobs' own death-as-decision-tool philosophy.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
