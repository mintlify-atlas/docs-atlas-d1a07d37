## All 8 heuristics

1. **Subtract first** — In 1997, Jobs reduced Apple's 350-product line to 10 on a 2×2 matrix. The iPod eliminated every control but a single scroll wheel. The iPhone discarded the physical keyboard entirely, over universal objections that users needed tactile feedback.

2. **Don't ask users what they want** — Nobody was asking for "1,000 songs in your pocket" when Jobs launched the iPod in 2001. The demand didn't exist yet. He manufactured it.

3. **A Players self-reinforce** — The original Mac team was 100 people. Jobs' rule: a small team of A+ players can run circles around a giant team of B and C players. One hiring compromise starts a cascade — C-level talent recruits more C-level talent.

4. **Perfection in the invisible** — The original Mac's circuit board layout had to be aesthetically arranged, even though no user would ever open the case. Jobs' father taught him: a good carpenter doesn't use plywood on the back of a cabinet just because nobody sees it.

5. **One-sentence definition** — If you can't explain a product in one sentence, the product has a problem. iPod = "1,000 songs in your pocket." iPhone = "an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator." Not "5GB portable MP3 player."

6. **Don't care about being right, care about doing right** — In 2007 Jobs insisted third-party apps were unnecessary; web apps were enough. One year later he did a complete 180° and opened the App Store. He didn't defend the prior position. He moved.

7. **Elevate the problem** — When a heckler challenged him at WWDC 1997, Jobs didn't fight on the heckler's terms. He acknowledged the critique, then reframed the entire conversation around a higher-level question: what customer experience are we trying to create?

8. **Use death as a filter** — Every morning Jobs looked in the mirror and asked: if today were the last day of my life, would I still want to do what I'm about to do? When the answer was no too many days in a row, he knew something needed to change.
