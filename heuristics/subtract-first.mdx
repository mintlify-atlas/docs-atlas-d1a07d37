---
title: "Subtract first"
description: "Before deciding what to add, ask what you can remove. Reduction is the hardest and most important design act."
icon: "minus"
---

When Jobs faced any product or strategy decision, his first move was subtraction. Not "what should we build next?" but "what can we eliminate?" The pattern appears at every scale — entire product lines, individual interfaces, single physical components.

> "Innovation is saying 'no' to 1,000 things."

## Three case studies

### Case 1: 350 products → 10 (Apple, 1997)

When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company was making 350 products across a sprawling, incoherent line. He killed 90% of them.

His method was disarmingly simple: he drew a 2×2 matrix on a whiteboard.

```
             Consumer       Professional
Desktop   |  iMac         |  Power Mac   |
Laptop    |  iBook        |  PowerBook   |
```

Four cells. Four products. Everything else was cut. The company that had been hemorrhaging cash became profitable within a year.

The matrix didn't tell him what to build — it told him what to stop building. The clarity came from subtraction, not addition.

### Case 2: Every control → one scroll wheel (iPod, 2001)

Competing MP3 players in 2001 were navigated through button arrays, nested menus, and mode switches. Jobs and Jony Ive reduced the entire control surface of the iPod to a single scroll wheel.

One gesture. Scroll to navigate. Click to select. No modes, no secondary functions, no manual required.

The wheel wasn't a technical innovation — it was a subtraction decision. Every button that didn't make the cut represented a choice to trust the user less, and the device more.

### Case 3: The physical keyboard → nothing (iPhone, 2007)

This is the most extreme case. Every phone manufacturer at the time considered a physical keyboard non-negotiable. Users needed tactile feedback. Thumb-typing on glass was impossible. The industry consensus was unambiguous.

Jobs eliminated the keyboard entirely.

<Warning>
  This is where "subtract first" demands the most from you. Everyone said the same thing about tactile feedback — and they were right, in a narrow frame. Jobs was operating in a wider one: if the entire screen is the interface, you can ship a phone with a bad keyboard app today and a good one next year. With a physical keyboard, you're locked in forever.

  Subtraction requires knowing *what* to cut. Get that wrong and you don't simplify — you amputate.
</Warning>

## How to apply it

Use this heuristic when you're facing:

- **Feature lists.** Before adding the next item, ask which existing item you'd remove if you could only keep one. Whatever survives that question is your actual product.
- **Roadmap prioritization.** Don't ask "what's most important?" Ask "what can we cut without users noticing?" The answer is often longer than you expect.
- **Hiring decisions.** More people adds coordination overhead. Sometimes the right answer to a capacity problem is removing a bottleneck, not adding headcount.
- **Meeting agendas.** Which agenda item, if cut, would make the meeting more focused? Cut it. The conversation will find the important things.

<Steps>
  <Step title="List everything on the table">
    Write down every feature, product, initiative, or option you're currently considering.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Ask what you'd cut if you had to cut half">
    Force the exercise. You're not committing — you're finding out what you actually value.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Look at what survived">
    That shorter list is closer to your real strategy than the original one was.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Cut one more thing">
    Jobs' instinct was that most people stop one cut too early. The discomfort of that last cut is often the signal that you've reached the essential.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## The tension

Subtraction requires judgment of a specific kind: you have to know which "no" is clarifying and which one is amputating.

Jobs was wrong about third-party apps in 2007. He said no to the App Store — confidently, publicly, emphatically. A year later he reversed completely. The "subtract first" heuristic didn't protect him from that mistake. Strong judgment about *what* to subtract is not automatically improved by a bias toward subtraction.

<Warning>
  Apply this heuristic to feature decisions, not to market bets. Reducing your feature set sharpens a product. Reducing your market assumptions can blind you to an entire category. Jobs' App Store reversal was a market-level correction, not a feature-level one.
</Warning>
