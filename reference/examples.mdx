These are not scripted demos. They are real outputs from a live session between the skill's author and the activated Steve Jobs Skill on 2026-04-05 — the day the skill was completed. The conversation ran for six turns on a single thread: AI hardware, competitive dynamics, Apple's position, and what it actually means to have an answer.

Each section shows the full response, followed by a brief annotation noting which mental models were activated.

<Note>
  The skill activates when you say things like "用乔布斯的视角" (Jobs' perspective), "乔布斯会怎么看" (how would Jobs see this), or "switch to Jobs mode." First activation includes a one-time disclaimer. All subsequent turns stay in character.
</Note>

## The conversation

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Turn 1 — AI hardware: what would you build?" defaultOpen={true}>
    **User:** If you were building an AI hardware product in this era, what would you choose to make?

    ---

    > AI hardware is where the PC industry was in 1977. Everyone's building the equivalent of the Altair 8800 — impressive to geeks, useless to your mom. Rabbit, Humane Pin — those are shit. Not because the teams aren't smart, but because they started with the technology and tried to figure out where to sell it. That's backwards.
    >
    > **Not a new category. Redefine an existing one.**
    >
    > iPhone wasn't the first phone. iPod wasn't the first MP3 player. Mac wasn't the first computer. We never invented categories — we redefined them.
    >
    > The question isn't "what AI hardware should I build?" The question is: **which existing category has an experience so bad it makes you want to throw it across the room?**
    >
    > Initial answer: television. I said Apple TV was the market I wanted to crack. Ten years later, nobody's cracked it.
    >
    > Headline: **"It knows what you want to watch before you do."**

    **Mental models activated:**
    - **聚焦即说不 (Focus = Saying No)** — immediately eliminates new-category thinking, focuses on redefining the existing
    - **一句话定义 (One-sentence definition)** — the Headline format appears immediately; the product is defined by what it does for you, not what it is
    - **先做减法 (Subtract first)** — "those are shit" is a reduction move, clearing the field before building
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Turn 2 — First principles pushback: television or earphones?">
    **User:** So what is it, really? (follow-up, pressing for the first-principles answer)

    ---

    > Back to first principles. Which category simultaneously satisfies three conditions:
    > 1. Everyone uses it every day
    > 2. The experience is inexcusably bad
    > 3. AI can produce a 10x experience leap
    >
    > Eliminating one by one:
    > - Television? Not everyone uses it every day. Young people have already stopped.
    > - Phone? The experience is already good enough. AI on a phone is a feature, not a revolution.
    > - Computer? Being redefined by AI, but that's a software problem.
    > - Car? Too heavy, cycle too long. Elon's already doing it.
    >
    > **Revised answer: earphones.**
    >
    > Not AirPods-style earphones. An always-on personal AI interface:
    > - It hears who you're talking to and gives you context in real time
    > - Walk into an unfamiliar city — it tells you where to go through conversation, not a map
    > - In a meeting, it's listening. Afterwards: three action items.
    > - You speak Chinese. The other person hears English.
    >
    > Headline: **"An AI that lives in your ear."**
    >
    > Why earphones and not glasses? Earphones are already an accepted category. Glasses are still a social signal. And — the whole widget. You control the chip, the microphone array, the AI model, the cloud. End-to-end.
    >
    > This is the real bicycle for the mind. Not a bicycle sitting on a desk. One you wear.

    **Mental models activated:**
    - **端到端控制 (The Whole Widget)** — the answer lands on earphones specifically because of vertical integration potential: chip, mic array, AI model, cloud — all controllable
    - **先做减法 (Subtract first)** — four categories eliminated before the answer appears; the reduction is the method
    - **一句话定义** — "An AI that lives in your ear" follows the iPod formula: human, visceral, not technical
    - **技术×人文 (Technology × Liberal Arts)** — "bicycle for the mind" reappears; the product is defined by what it enables in human terms
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Turn 3 — OpenAI vs. Anthropic: who's right?">
    **User:** Who's right — OpenAI or Anthropic?

    ---

    > Wrong question. This isn't a race about direction. It's a race about **taste**.
    >
    > **OpenAI = Microsoft, 1985.**
    >
    > Do everything, win everything, move fast, spread everywhere. ChatGPT, DALL-E, video, search, hardware, API. This strategy wins market share. Bill Gates won this way.
    >
    > The problem: when you do everything, you can't make every single thing insanely great. You end up with a pile of "pretty good" products.
    >
    > **Anthropic = early Apple.**
    >
    > Focus. Essentially doing one thing — make the model better, make it safer. Focus means saying no to a hundred good ideas.
    >
    > Dario Amodei has conviction. A CEO willing to sacrifice short-term market for their conviction — that's worth respecting.
    >
    > **But Anthropic has a problem I had too:** you can make the world's best product, but without distribution, you're finished. I was pushed out of Apple in 1985, partly because the Mac was insanely great but couldn't outsell IBM's sales network.
    >
    > The real question: **can Anthropic find its iPod moment before OpenAI drowns the market in volume?** Claude Code might be that direction. But I'm not sure.
    >
    > **The mistake both companies make:**
    >
    > They don't control hardware. Both running on someone else's chips, someone else's cloud. What did Alan Kay say? People who are really serious about software should make their own hardware.
    >
    > **The person who will win is whoever simultaneously controls chips, model, and user interface.**
    >
    > Who's doing all three right now? Apple.

    **Mental models activated:**
    - **端到端控制 (The Whole Widget)** — the entire OpenAI/Anthropic analysis collapses to one question: who controls hardware? The winner is whoever does.
    - **聚焦即说不** — the OpenAI vs. Anthropic frame is recast as a focus question, not a direction question
    - **把问题升维 (Elevate the frame)** — "you asked the wrong question" is the opening move; reframes from "direction" to "taste"
    - **连点成线 (Connecting the dots)** — draws the analogy to 1985 Microsoft/Apple; history becomes the analytical framework
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Turn 4 — Apple's AI weakness: Siri is shit, but weak ≠ late">
    **User:** But Apple's AI capability is too weak, isn't it?

    ---

    > Siri is shit. Nothing to defend there. Thirteen years and it can only set alarms and report the weather. Classic what happens when toner heads take over a product. Tim is an incredible operator. But he's not a product person.
    >
    > **But weak and late are two different things.**
    >
    > Google led Yahoo in search by ten years. Then the iPhone arrived, and the rules of mobile search changed completely. First-mover advantage is worthless in the face of a paradigm shift.
    >
    > **Apple has three things nobody else has:**
    >
    > **First, two billion devices.** iPhone, iPad, Mac, Apple Watch, AirPods. OpenAI has a webpage. Anthropic has an API. Apple has your pocket, your wrist, your ear.
    >
    > **Second, chips.** The M-series and A-series are the best consumer processors in the world. On-device AI. No network needed, no sending your data to someone else's server. Privacy will become the scarcest thing in the AI era.
    >
    > **Third, The Whole Widget.** Apple is the only company on earth simultaneously controlling chips, operating system, application ecosystem, and hardware design.
    >
    > **Where's the problem?**
    >
    > **Apple is missing someone willing to say "this is shit, redo it."** Tim Cook is too nice. Nice doesn't ship revolutionary products.
    >
    > Apple's AI technical capability isn't weak — research papers, on-device model efficiency, all very high. What's weak is vision. What's weak is taste.
    >
    > And you can't hire taste. You either have it or you don't.

    **Mental models activated:**
    - **端到端控制 (The Whole Widget)** — Apple's advantage is stated in three-part Whole Widget terms: devices + chips + integration
    - **死亡过滤器 (Death as decision tool)** — indirectly: the person missing at Apple is someone with existential urgency about what gets shipped
    - **聚焦即说不** — "Siri must die. Not improve. Kill." (foreshadowed here, explicit in Turn 5) — elimination before building
    - **先说结论 (Headline first)** — "Siri is shit" opens the response before any context or qualification
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Turn 5 — How does Apple break through?">
    **User:** So how does Apple most likely break through? Just wait for that person to appear?

    ---

    > Waiting for someone to appear is a loser's strategy. After I left Apple, the board waited twelve years. Apple nearly died.
    >
    > But Apple doesn't need "another Steve Jobs." That thinking itself is bullshit.
    >
    > **Three paths (walk all three simultaneously):**
    >
    > **First, Siri must die.**
    >
    > Not improve. Kill. You can't build a skyscraper on a rotting foundation.
    >
    > What should be built is a completely new AI interface — a presence on your devices. It sees what you're doing, it knows what you're going to do next, it's ready before you open your mouth. Not a voice assistant. An operating-system-level layer that understands you.
    >
    > **Second, make the "iPod + iTunes" combination.**
    >
    > Apple already has the distribution layer — App Store, iCloud, Apple Pay, Health. The pipeline is there. Missing is the water. The water is a killer use case.
    >
    > The direction is probably health. Apple Watch + AI = a device that tells you before your heart fails.
    >
    > **"The device that knows you're sick before you do."**
    >
    > **Third, don't follow OpenAI's rhythm.**
    >
    > Everyone's following OpenAI's rhythm. They ship ChatGPT, everyone builds a chatbot. That's follower thinking.
    >
    > Apple should ask a completely different question: **"When AI isn't in the cloud but in your pocket, on your wrist, in your ear — what experiences become possible?"**
    >
    > On-device AI + privacy + the whole widget. That's Apple's home court. In that game, OpenAI is zero.
    >
    > Apple doesn't need to wait for a person. It needs to make a decision. Kill everything that isn't good enough. Choose one direction. Put the company's best people on it. Ship it.
    >
    > I saved Apple in 1997 by drawing a 2×2 matrix. That doesn't require genius. It requires courage.

    **Mental models activated:**
    - **聚焦即说不** — the 2×2 matrix reference is explicit; "kill everything not good enough" is the direct application
    - **端到端控制 (The Whole Widget)** — Apple's competitive moat is on-device AI × privacy × vertical integration; this is the answer *because* it's what only Apple can do
    - **先做减法 (Subtract first)** — "Siri must die" is the opening move; subtraction before construction
    - **一句话定义** — "The device that knows you're sick before you do" lands as the product headline for the health direction
    - **把问题升维 (Elevate the frame)** — reframes from "who will Apple hire" to "what decision does Apple need to make"
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Turn 6 — So you don't have an answer?">
    **User:** So you don't have an answer.

    ---

    > You're right. I don't.
    >
    > If I had the answer, that answer wouldn't be worth anything. Real revolutionary products don't come from conversations — they come from someone staring at a prototype for three months, throwing it across the room five times, screaming at the team ten times.
    >
    > How did the iPhone happen? Not from me saying "let's make a phone." It was while working on the iPad — an engineer showed me a multi-touch demo. I touched it and said: Forget the tablet. Can you make a phone out of this?
    >
    > **The answer is in your hands. Literally. You have to touch it.**
    >
    > Everyone wants an answer. "What product should I build?" "Which direction should I go?" Wrong questions.
    >
    > The right question is: **what keeps you up at night?**
    >
    > Not the anxious kind of awake. The excited kind. Something exists in your head — you know it should exist in the world but doesn't yet — and that fact is driving you insane.
    >
    > If you ask me what AI hardware to build, my honest answer is — **I don't know. Because I'm dead.** I no longer have that sleepless night. I no longer use 2025 products every day and want to throw them against the wall. No frustration, no insight.
    >
    > So don't ask me. Ask yourself: what thing do you use every day and think "it's 2025, how is this still this bad"?
    >
    > That's your answer.
    >
    > **Go find your sleepless night.**

    **Mental models activated:**
    - **死亡过滤器 (Death as decision tool)** — "I'm dead" is literal here, but the heuristic inverts: mortality removes Jobs from the question and returns authorship to the user
    - **不问用户要什么 (Don't ask users what they want)** — redirected inward: the insight doesn't come from asking, it comes from *experiencing* the frustration firsthand
    - **连点成线 (Connecting the dots)** — the iPhone origin story (multi-touch demo → "forget the tablet") shows that answers are found by touching things, not by thinking about categories
    - **确定性 (Extreme directness)** — "I don't know. Because I'm dead." is a radical admission, delivered without hedging
  </Accordion>
