The Steve Jobs Skill is grounded in documentary evidence, not mythology. Every mental model, heuristic, and expression pattern in SKILL.md is traceable to a specific source. This page catalogs that evidence base.

<Note>
  Research conducted: **2026-04-05**. All claims in the skill were cross-validated across multiple sources before distillation. Any claim present in only one source was marked as lower-confidence or excluded.
</Note>

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Primary sources">
    Primary sources are Jobs' direct output — his own words in speeches, interviews, and published writing. These carry the highest evidentiary weight because they show Jobs' thinking in real time, rather than through others' interpretation.

    ### Stanford Commencement Address, 2005

    Delivered June 12, 2005 at Stanford University. Three stories: connecting the dots (Reed College / calligraphy), love and loss (being fired from Apple, Pixar, NeXT), and death (using mortality as a decision tool).

    Available at: [stevejobsarchive.com](https://stevejobsarchive.com) and Stanford's official archive.

    **Key passages distilled into the skill:**
    - "You can't connect the dots looking forward; you can only connect them looking backwards."
    - "If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I am about to do today?"
    - "Stay Hungry. Stay Foolish."

    ---

    ### Make Something Wonderful (Steve Jobs Archive, 2023)

    A posthumous collection published by the Steve Jobs Archive, edited by Laurene Powell Jobs. Contains letters, speeches, and interviews not previously published. The most complete single-volume record of Jobs' written voice.

    Available at: [stevejobsarchive.com/book](https://stevejobsarchive.com/book)

    ---

    ### The Lost Interview with Bob Cringely, 1995 (PBS)

    Filmed for the PBS documentary *Triumph of the Nerds* but largely cut from the broadcast version. The full interview was thought lost until a VHS copy was found in 2011 and released as a standalone film.

    This is the most candid long-form interview Jobs gave. He discusses Microsoft, what went wrong at Apple, his management philosophy, and his beliefs about innovation — without the polish of a keynote or the constraints of a product launch context.

    **Key passages:**
    - "I don't really care about being right. I just care about success."
    - Extended analysis of why Apple lost to Microsoft — and why it was partly Jobs' own fault
    - First clear articulation of what would become the "墨粉脑袋 (toner heads)" theory of corporate decline

    ---

    ### D Conference series (D3, D5, D8) — AllThingsD

    Walt Mossberg and Kara Swisher's interview series at the D: All Things Digital conference. Jobs appeared multiple times, most notably:

    - **D3 (2005)** — First joint appearance with Bill Gates, discussing the history of personal computing
    - **D5 (2007)** — Second joint appearance with Gates, one week before the iPhone launched to the public
    - **D8 (2010)** — Solo appearance, one of his last major long-form interviews. Covered the iPad, Flash vs. HTML5, and his views on privacy

    All transcripts and videos available via All Things Digital archive.

    ---

    ### WWDC Keynotes and Q&As, 1997–2011

    Jobs' annual developer conference appearances are the primary source for understanding his product philosophy as it evolved in real time. The 1997 WWDC is particularly important — it contains the most explicit on-record statement of the "Focus = Saying No" model, delivered in response to a hostile audience question.

    **Key passages:**
    - WWDC 1997: "People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are."
    - WWDC 1997: "You've got to start with the customer experience and work backwards to the technology."

    ---

    ### Thoughts on Music (2007)

    An open letter published by Jobs on Apple.com arguing against DRM (digital rights management) in music. A rare example of Jobs making a public argument in writing, showing his rhetorical structure outside of a keynote format.

    ---

    ### Thoughts on Flash (2010)

    An open letter published on Apple.com explaining Apple's decision not to support Adobe Flash on the iPhone and iPad. Became one of the most discussed technology policy documents of its era.

    **Why it matters for the skill:** This letter demonstrates the "升维 (elevate the frame)" heuristic in written form. Rather than debating Flash's technical merits, Jobs reframed the entire argument around what was good for the future of the web.

    ---

    ### iPhone Keynote, January 9, 2007 — Macworld

    The single most-studied product launch presentation in technology history. The 80-minute presentation has been analyzed by rhetoricians, product designers, and business schools.

    **Structural elements distilled into the skill's "Expression DNA" section:**
    - Three-part problem statement → reveal that it's one product
    - "Are you getting it?" — audience involvement before the reveal
    - The "1,000 songs in your pocket" model applied to phone: "an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator"
    - Dramatic pause before each reveal
    - Specific, verifiable claims rather than general superlatives

    ---

    ### Playboy Interview, 1985

    An extended interview published in *Playboy* shortly before Jobs was forced out of Apple. Contains his most direct early statements about design, technology, and his vision for personal computing.

    **Key passages:**
    - The "bicycle for the mind" metaphor — first major public appearance of this frame
    - Early articulation of the software-hardware integration philosophy

    ---

    ### Apple Newsroom Resignation Letter, 2011

    Jobs' formal letter to the Apple board announcing his resignation as CEO. Terse, precise, and completely without self-pity. "I have always said if there ever came a day when I could no longer meet my duties and expectations as Apple's CEO, I would be the first to let you know. Unfortunately, that day has come."

  <Tab title="Secondary sources">
    Secondary sources are works by others — biographers, colleagues, journalists, and researchers — who documented, analyzed, or directly observed Jobs. They provide context, correction, and perspectives that Jobs' own public statements cannot.

    ### Walter Isaacson, *Steve Jobs* (2011)

    The authorized biography, commissioned by Jobs himself. Isaacson conducted 40+ direct interviews with Jobs over two years, plus interviews with more than 100 family members, friends, colleagues, competitors, and adversaries.

    **Reliability note:** Because Jobs authorized the biography, some accounts reflect his preferred framing. Isaacson was aware of this and included critical perspectives from people Jobs had wronged or dismissed. The biography is the most comprehensive single source but should be cross-validated against Folklore.org and *Becoming Steve Jobs* for balance.

    **Key content distilled into the skill:**
    - The Reality Distortion Field — Bud Tribble's account of working under Jobs in 1981
    - The Lisa daughter denial and eventual reconciliation
    - The 9-month medical treatment delay (the most significant example of RDF being self-applied)
    - The Pixar acquisition story and what Jobs learned from storytelling

    ---

    ### Brent Schlender & Rick Tetzeli, *Becoming Steve Jobs* (2015)

    A corrective biography written by a journalist who knew Jobs personally for 25 years. Schlender argues that Isaacson's portrait overemphasizes the "genius tyrant" narrative and underweights Jobs' growth as a leader during his Pixar years.

    **Why it matters:** *Becoming Steve Jobs* is the primary source for understanding the decade between Apple exile and Apple return (1985–1997) — the period when Jobs' most important learning happened. The skill's "internal tensions" section draws heavily on Schlender's more nuanced account.

    ---

    ### Andy Hertzfeld, Folklore.org

    A firsthand record of the original Macintosh development team, written by Andy Hertzfeld — one of the original Mac engineers. Contains hundreds of specific stories about Jobs' behavior, decision-making, and interpersonal style from people who worked with him directly in 1981–1984.

    Available at: [folklore.org](https://folklore.org)

    **Why it matters:** Folklore.org is the most granular record of Jobs' actual behavior in a product development context — as opposed to his public statements. The "invisible quality" heuristic (circuit board aesthetics), the Reality Distortion Field naming, and numerous specific product decisions are documented here.

    ---

    ### Carmine Gallo, *The Presentation Secrets of Steve Jobs* (2010)

    A rhetorical analysis of Jobs' keynote presentation technique, structured as a practical guide. Gallo reverse-engineered Jobs' presentation style across dozens of keynotes.

    **Key content distilled:** The "Expression DNA" section of SKILL.md draws on Gallo's analysis of Jobs' specific rhetorical techniques: the rule of three, the villain-hero narrative structure, the use of a single headline number, and the "one more thing" close.

    ---

    ### European Rhetoric — iPhone Keynote rhetorical analysis

    Academic analysis of the January 9, 2007 Macworld keynote from a classical rhetoric perspective. Identifies Jobs' use of *ethos* (credibility through specific technical knowledge), *pathos* (emotional narrative framing), and *logos* (logical product comparisons) in ways that align with but predate Gallo's popular analysis.

    ---

    ### Harvard Business Review — leadership case studies

    Multiple HBR case studies on Apple's product strategy and Jobs' leadership style, covering the 1997 return, the iPod-iTunes integration decision, and the App Store reversal. Used as secondary validation for the "decision analysis" content in SKILL.md.

    ---

    ### Public statements from colleagues and adversaries

    Statements from people who worked with or competed against Jobs, used to triangulate between Jobs' self-presentation and how he was experienced by others:

    - **Bill Gates** — praised Jobs' product taste while noting their fundamental disagreement about open vs. closed platforms
    - **Tim Cook** — multiple interviews post-2011 discussing what Jobs taught him and what he deliberately did not try to replicate
    - **Jony Ive** — extensive public statements about the design philosophy he developed with Jobs; the most reliable source on Jobs' actual design process
    - **Steve Wozniak** — candid about Jobs' limitations as an engineer and what Woz contributed that Jobs could not
    - **John Lasseter (Pixar)** — on Jobs' evolution as a leader during the Pixar years

    ### How this skill was built

    The Steve Jobs Skill was generated by [女娲.skill](https://github.com/alchaincyf/nuwa-skill) — an automated skill-distillation system that runs six parallel research agents, cross-validates findings, and distills verified claims into a structured SKILL.md.

    ### Six parallel research agents

    Each agent focused on a distinct dimension of Jobs' public record:

    | Agent | Focus | Output |
    |-------|-------|--------|
    | **01-writings** | Books, speeches, open letters — Jobs' written and prepared output | 359 lines |
    | **02-conversations** | Long-form interviews, Q&As, conference appearances — Jobs' unscripted thinking | 489 lines |
    | **03-expression-dna** | Keynote rhetorical analysis, email style, the Reality Distortion Field mechanism | 444 lines |
    | **04-external-views** | Ive, Cook, Wozniak, Gates, Lasseter — how others experienced Jobs | 464 lines |
    | **05-decisions** | 15 major decisions analyzed by background, logic, outcome, and retrospective assessment | 452 lines |
    | **06-timeline** | Complete life timeline 1955–2011 plus relationship maps | 289 lines |

    Total raw research: **2,497 lines** across 6 files, available in [`references/research/`](https://github.com/alchaincyf/steve-jobs-skill/tree/main/references/research).

    ### Cross-validation protocol

    Before any claim was distilled into SKILL.md, it had to appear in at least two independent sources. The only exceptions were:

    1. Direct quotes with clear attribution to a specific public event (e.g., WWDC 1997 transcript)
    2. Firsthand accounts from Folklore.org with named authors

    Claims that appeared in only one source were marked as lower-confidence or excluded entirely.

    ### Quality validation

    After the skill was assembled, it was tested against four categories:

    - **3 known test cases** — questions with documented historical answers (e.g., "what did Jobs say about focus at WWDC 1997?")
    - **1 edge case** — a question at the boundary of available evidence
    - **1 style test** — does the output match Jobs' actual expression patterns?

    ### Explicitly excluded sources

    The following source categories were excluded from research and are not represented in SKILL.md:

    - 知乎 (Zhihu) posts and threads
    - WeChat public account articles (微信公众号)
    - Baidu Baike entries (百度百科)
    - Unattributed "Jobs quotes" circulating on social media
    - Books and articles that do not cite primary sources

    The exclusion of these sources is not a statement about their general reliability — it reflects the specific standard required for a skill claiming to represent a historical figure's cognitive framework. Every claim here is traceable to a named source with a clear evidentiary basis.

    ### Known limitations

    The skill itself documents five explicit limitations:

    1. **Cannot replicate Jobs' creative intuition** — the framework provides thinking tools, not creative genius
    2. **Public expression ≠ private reasoning** — Jobs was a skilled communicator whose public statements were often designed for effect
    3. **Frozen in 2011** — Jobs died before AI, cloud computing's dominance, and social media's present form. Any statements about these topics are extrapolation
    4. **Management style is context-dependent** — Jobs' approach worked in specific cultural and organizational conditions; direct replication may cause harm in other contexts
    5. **Survivorship bias** — the skill necessarily emphasizes successful decisions more than failures, because successful decisions are better documented
