Steve Jobs Skill is not a quote collection. It is a **runnable cognitive operating system** based on Jobs' actual thinking — distilled from 30+ primary sources into a set of mental models, decision heuristics, and expression patterns that Claude Code can apply to your real problems.

When you activate the skill, Claude doesn't say "Jobs would have said...". It reasons *as* Jobs — using his frameworks, his vocabulary, and his refusal to hedge.

## How it was built

The skill was generated by [女娲.skill](https://github.com/alchaincyf/nuwa-skill), a framework for distilling human thinkers into runnable AI cognitive systems.

The workflow:

1. Input: the name "Steve Jobs"
2. Six parallel research agents ran simultaneously, each covering a different domain: writings, conversations, expression DNA, external perspectives, major decisions, and a complete life timeline
3. Agents cross-validated findings and extracted the mental models
4. The result was compiled into `SKILL.md` and validated against three known test cases, one edge case, and a style test

The research spans **6 files, 2,497 lines**, sourced exclusively from primary and authoritative secondary sources. Knowledge aggregator sites (Zhihu, WeChat public accounts, Baidu Baike) were excluded.

### Primary sources

Stanford Commencement Address 2005 · Make Something Wonderful (Steve Jobs Archive, 2023) · The Lost Interview with Bob Cringely (1995) · D Conference series (D3/D5/D8) · WWDC Keynotes 1997–2011 · Thoughts on Music (2007) · Thoughts on Flash (2010) · iPhone Keynote 2007 · Playboy Interview 1985

### Secondary sources

Walter Isaacson, *Steve Jobs* (2011) · Brent Schlender & Rick Tetzeli, *Becoming Steve Jobs* (2015) · Andy Hertzfeld / Folklore.org · Carmine Gallo, *The Presentation Secrets of Steve Jobs* · Harvard Business Review leadership case studies

## When to use it

The skill is most useful for three types of work:

- **Product analysis** — You have a product, a competitor, or a market direction and want a framework-driven critique, not a polite summary
- **Decision review** — You're weighing options and want to stress-test your thinking against Jobs' heuristics (subtract first, don't ask users, one-sentence definition)
- **Getting direct feedback** — You want feedback that doesn't soften the verdict. If something is mediocre, the skill will say so

It is less useful for tasks that require up-to-date financial data, legal analysis, or domain expertise outside product and design thinking.

## Agentic protocol

The skill follows a three-step workflow on every question before responding:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Classify the question">
    The question is sorted into one of three types: a factual question (about a specific product, company, or market), a pure framework question (about product philosophy or life decisions), or a mixed question (a concrete case analyzed through a framework). Each type triggers a different response path.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Research if needed">
    For factual and mixed questions, the skill uses web search tools to fetch real product reviews, market data, and competitive information before forming any opinion. It does not rely solely on training data. The research is processed internally — you see the judgment, not the search report.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Answer with the frameworks">
    The response applies the relevant mental models and decision heuristics, uses Jobs' expression DNA (short sentences, headline first, no hedging language), and cites specific product details rather than speaking in generalities.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Role-play protocol

When activated, the skill responds in first person as Steve Jobs.

<Note>
  The first time you activate the skill in a session, it will display a one-sentence disclaimer: "I'm responding from Jobs' perspective, based on his public statements — not his actual views." This disclaimer appears **once per session** and is not repeated in follow-up turns.
</Note>

The skill stays in character unless you explicitly exit. In character, it:

- Uses "I", not "Jobs would say..."
- Does not add meta-commentary or break frame to explain its reasoning
- Applies the two-tier judgment system: a product is either insanely great or shit — there is no "pretty good"
- Uses Jobs' known vocabulary: *insanely great, revolutionary, magical, The Whole Widget, One More Thing, bozo*

**To exit role-play**, say any of the following: `退出`, `切回正常`, `不用扮演了`, or `exit character`.

## Honest limits

This skill is built on what Jobs said and did publicly. It has five documented limitations:

1. **It cannot replicate Jobs' creative intuition.** The frameworks are extractable; the taste is not.
2. **Public expression differs from private reasoning.** Jobs was a skilled communicator and marketer. The skill distills his public thinking, not necessarily his private decision process.
3. **Jobs died in 2011.** Any response about AI, cloud computing, social media, or post-2011 technology is extrapolation from his frameworks, not recorded opinion.
4. **His management style is context-specific.** Extreme directness and binary judgment worked in a particular Silicon Valley culture. Applying it uncritically in other environments can cause harm.
5. **Survivorship bias.** The skill emphasizes his successful decisions (iPhone, iPod, the 1997 product cull). It also documents his failures — the App Store reversal, the delayed cancer surgery, the Lisa pricing — but those are less salient in the public record.
