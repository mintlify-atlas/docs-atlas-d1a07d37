---
title: "Reality Distortion Field"
description: "Push teams beyond their perceived limits by making the impossible feel inevitable."
---

> "In his presence, reality is malleable. He can convince anyone of practically anything."

## One-sentence summary

Many "impossible" constraints are not physical limits — they are the outer edge of what a team has previously been asked to do.

## Primary evidence

<Note>
  **Bud Tribble, 1981** — The term "Reality Distortion Field" was coined by Bud Tribble, a software engineer on the original Mac team. He borrowed it from a *Star Trek: The Original Series* episode ("The Menagerie") in which aliens construct a false reality through sheer mental force. Tribble used it to describe what happened when Jobs entered a room.

  **Andy Hertzfeld, Folklore.org** — Hertzfeld, another original Mac team member, documented it this way:

  > "The reality distortion field was a confounding mélange of a charismatic rhetorical style, an indomitable will, and an eagerness to bend any fact to fit the purpose at hand... In his presence, reality is malleable. He can convince anyone of practically anything. It wears off when he's not around, but it makes it hard to know if he's leading you off a cliff or if you really can do the impossible."
</Note>

### Mac team: shipping on "impossible" deadlines

The original Macintosh team was given a product development timeline that experienced engineers said was impossible. Jobs did not adjust the timeline. He adjusted the team's belief about what was possible.

The machine shipped. The engineers later described the experience as one of the most demanding and most meaningful of their careers — often in the same sentence.

### iPhone: a new product category in 18 months

When Jobs decided Apple would build a phone, the engineering challenges were genuinely unprecedented: a full touchscreen OS, mobile networking, a battery that could last a day, miniaturized hardware that had never been combined before. The standard industry estimate for a project of that complexity was 3-5 years.

The iPhone launched in January 2007, approximately 18 months after serious development began. It redefined an entire industry.

Jobs did not do this by ignoring reality. He did it by refusing to accept that the initial assessment of what was possible was final.

## How to apply it

<Steps>
  <Step title="Distinguish limits from priors">
    When a team says "we can't do this in that timeframe," ask whether that is a physical constraint (laws of physics, hard dependencies) or a prior (it's never been done in that time before, we don't know how to do it yet). Most "impossibles" are priors.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Challenge the framing, not just the schedule">
    Jobs didn't push teams by demanding the same approach faster. He pushed by questioning whether the approach itself was right. Often the path that seemed impossible via approach A was achievable via approach B — which only became visible when approach A was rejected as insufficient.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Be present">
    The RDF operated in person. Hertzfeld noted it wore off when Jobs wasn't around. The model requires sustained engagement with the team and the work, not just an ambitious mandate sent by memo.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  The model works best when you genuinely believe the goal is achievable. Teams can distinguish between a leader who has thought through why something is possible and a leader who simply hasn't accepted the constraints. The RDF was convincing partly because Jobs had usually done the homework.
</Tip>

## The dark side

<Warning>
  **Jobs used the RDF on himself, with serious consequences.**

  In October 2003, Jobs was diagnosed with a rare form of pancreatic cancer. His doctors told him surgery was necessary. Jobs refused, and spent nine months trying alternative treatments — special diets, acupuncture, herbal remedies, spiritual consultations. He later told Walter Isaacson he believed he could cure himself through methods his doctors considered ineffective.

  By the time he agreed to surgery in July 2004, the cancer had spread. His biographer and his oncologist both concluded the delay likely shortened his life.

  This is the cost of the Reality Distortion Field applied inward: the same cognitive pattern that pushed teams through "impossible" engineering problems convinced Jobs that the laws of oncology didn't apply to him. He described this as possibly the worst decision of his life.

  For teams: the RDF has a documented track record of enabling exceptional output and a documented track record of breaking people. Some Mac team members describe the experience as the peak of their careers; others describe it as causing serious personal damage. The model does not come with a dial that distinguishes productive push from destructive pressure.
</Warning>
