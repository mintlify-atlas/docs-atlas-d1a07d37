> "Innovation is saying 'no' to 1,000 things."

## One-sentence summary

Focus is not a positive act of commitment — it is a relentless act of elimination.

## Primary evidence

<Note>
  **WWDC 1997 Q&A** — This is the definitive statement. Jobs had just returned to Apple after 12 years. An audience member had publicly insulted him. His response reframed the entire question:

  > "People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are. You have to pick carefully. I'm actually as proud of the things we haven't done as the things I have done."
</Note>

### The 350→10 product cut (1997)

When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company had over 350 products across its lineup — printers, servers, PDAs, monitors, multiple overlapping Mac lines. Apple was 90 days from bankruptcy.

Jobs drew a 2×2 matrix on a whiteboard: Consumer vs. Professional on one axis, Desktop vs. Portable on the other. Four quadrants. One product per quadrant. He killed everything else.

350 products became 10. The four survivors: the iMac, the PowerMac G3, the iBook, and the PowerBook G3. That decision is directly traceable to Apple's return to profitability within a year.

This was not cost-cutting. It was the application of focus as a design principle to a product portfolio.

## How to apply it

<Steps>
  <Step title="Invert the question">
    When building a product feature list or strategic priority stack, don't ask "what should we add?" Ask: "what should we refuse to do?"
  </Step>
  <Step title="Force a matrix">
    If you can't draw a simple 2×2 or list your top 3 priorities on an index card, you haven't focused. Complexity is a sign that no one has made a real decision.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Apply to resource allocation">
    Every engineering hour spent on feature #11 is an hour not spent making feature #1 insanely great. Focus is a trade, not a limit.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  The hardest part of this model is that the things you say No to are often genuinely good ideas. The model only works if you accept that good is the enemy of great. "No to a thousand things" means no to things that would have shipped and worked.
</Tip>

## Known limitation

<Warning>
  **Jobs got this wrong with the App Store.** In 2007, he insisted that the iPhone would not support third-party native apps. Developers would build "web apps" through Safari. His reasoning was consistent with the Whole Widget model: he didn't want other people's software degrading the iPhone experience.

  He was wrong. Developers pushed back hard. By March 2008, Jobs had done a complete reversal and announced the App Store. It launched in July 2008 and became one of the most important platforms in the history of computing.

  The lesson: saying No requires judgment, not just will. A reflex to eliminate can eliminate the right thing for the wrong reasons. When Jobs applied this model to the App Store, he was protecting quality — but he misidentified where the quality threat actually lived.
</Warning>
