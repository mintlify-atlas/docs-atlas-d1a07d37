Jobs didn't operate from abstract philosophy. The six models below are patterns extracted from his actual decisions, statements, and product choices — each with primary source citations. They are not life advice. They are compression functions: ways of collapsing complex situations into a clear direction.

<Note>
  All models are sourced from Jobs' direct statements (WWDC keynotes, Stanford 2005 commencement address, the Lost Interview, D Conference series) or from firsthand accounts by people who worked directly with him (Andy Hertzfeld at Folklore.org, Bud Tribble, Walter Isaacson's authorized biography with 40+ direct interviews).
</Note>

## The 6 models

  <Card title="Focus = Saying No" icon="ban" href="/mental-models/focus-saying-no">

  <Card title="The Whole Widget" icon="layer-group" href="/mental-models/whole-widget">

  <Card title="Connecting the Dots" icon="circle-nodes" href="/mental-models/connecting-dots">
    You can't connect the dots looking forward; you can only connect them looking backwards. The Stanford 2005 speech as primary source, with three concrete case studies.

  <Card title="Death as Decision Tool" icon="hourglass-end" href="/mental-models/death-filter">

## How to use these models

Each model page follows the same structure:

- **One-sentence summary** — the distilled claim
- **Primary evidence** — direct quotes and documented decisions with sources
- **How to apply it** — the practical use case
- **Known limitation** — where Jobs himself got it wrong, or where the model breaks down

<Warning>
  These models reflect Jobs' public record. His public statements were carefully constructed — they represent his reasoning as he chose to present it, not necessarily every nuance of his private decision-making. Read the limitations sections. He was wrong more often than the mythology admits.
</Warning>

## What makes these different from quote collections

Most "Jobs wisdom" content is a list of quotes with no causal structure. These models answer a different question: **in what situations did Jobs invoke this pattern, and what did it cause him to do?**

The calligraphy course at Reed College is not just a story about following your passion. It is evidence for a specific claim about how to allocate attention when the ROI is invisible. That's a mental model. A quote is not.
